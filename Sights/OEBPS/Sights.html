Beijing is a city layered by time. Three thousand years as a settlement, eight                                          hundred as a capital – its history has never been broken, and its architecture wing                            breathes. The following five World Heritage sites are the city’s five business cards to                                  the world. They are not cold stone and brick, but time frozen solid, history you can see.                   

The Forbidden City

Welcome  to   Beijing 

Construction of the Forbidden City began in the fourth year of the Yongle reign of the Ming dynasty (1406 CE) and took fourteen years to complete. It then served as the residence of twenty-four emperors of the Ming and Qing dynasties - six hundred years of dynastic rise and fall, all contained within its 720,000 square meters of red walls and golden tiles. The three great halls - the Hall of Supreme Harmony, the Hall of Central Harmony, and the Hall of Preserving Harmony - sit firmly on the central axis, flanked by the Wenhua (Literary Glory) and Wuying (Martial Prowess) wings. More than 9,000 rooms unfold in layers; walking from the Meridian Gate (Wumen) all the way to the Gate of Divine Might (Shenwumen) is to walk through the power and glory of an entire dynasty. The architectural soul of the Forbidden City lies in its perfect symmetrical layout, aligned precisely with Beijing’s Central Axis - the majesty of the three Outer Court halls and the grace of the three Inner Court halls, laid out along a 7.8-kilometer axis that embodies the ideal of “establishing the palace at the center of the cosmos.”It is the largest and best-preserved surviving complex of wooden palace architecture in the world, listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and often called “the first of the world’s five great palaces”.

· Address: No. 4 Jingshan Qianjie, Dongcheng District

· Opening Hours: April 1 - October 31: 08:30-7:00 (last entry 16:10); November 1 -March 31: 08:30-6:30 (last entry 15:40). Closed on Mondays (except public holidays).

· Tickets: Peak season 60 RMB, off-season 40 RMB. Advance real-name reservation required via the “Forbidden City”WeChat mini-program.

The Great Wall

The Great Wall was not built in a single day, nor could it have been completed in any single dynasty. The sections of the Wall within Beijing stretch for 520 kilometers, spanning six districts: Pinggu, Miyun, Huairou, Yanqing, Changping, and Mentougou. Among them, Badaling is the earliest to open to the public and the most famous. The name "Badaling" means "roads lead in all directions," which itself speaks to its central role in military defense. During the Longqing reign of the Ming dynasty, the famous general Qi Jiguang oversaw the construction of hollow enemy watchtowers that ride on the wall itself, forming a defense system of towering walls and scattered watchtowers. Walking on the wall, with grey bricks underfoot and rolling ridges on either side, you occasionally enter a watchtower and feel a gust of wind pass through - a wind that may still carry the smoke of signal fires from five centuries ago.

· Location: Badaling Town, Yanqing District (Badaling Great Wall Scenic Area)

· Opening Hours: Peak season 06:30-6:30 (Badaling); off-season hours vary. Please check before your visit.

· Tickets: Peak season 40 RMB, off-season 35 RMB. Advance real-name reservation required via the "Badaling Great Wall" WeChat official account.

The Temple of Heaven

If the Forbidden City was the earthly residence of the emperor, the Temple of Heaven was where the emperor spoke with heaven. Construction began in the 18th year of the Yongle reign of the Ming dynasty (1420 CE), making it one year older than the Forbidden City. It covers 2.73 million square meters - about four times the size of the Forbidden City. The northern wall is semi-circular and the southern wall square, symbolizing "round heaven, square earth." From the Circular Mound Altar (Yuanqiu Tan) in the south to the Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests (Qinian Dian) in the north, a 30-meter-wide sacred way called the Danbi Bridge connects them. When the emperor walked this path to perform sacrifices, every step was solemn and reverent. The Temple of Heaven is the largest and best-preserved ancient sacrificial architectural complex in the world. It was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1998.

· Location: No. 1 Tiantan Road, Dongcheng District

· Opening Hours: Peak season 06:00-1:00 (site closes at 22:00); off-season 06:30-1:00. Core attractions such as the Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests and the Circular Mound Altar open at 08:00.

· Tickets: Peak season 15 RMB, off-season 10 RMB (entrance only); combined ticket 34 RMB. Please check the latest opening information before your visit.

The Summer Palace

The Summer Palace is a garden, and also a dream. In the 15th year of the Qianlong reign of the Qing dynasty (1750 CE), Emperor Qianlong took West Lake in Hangzhou as his model, drew on the garden design techniques of the Jiangnan region, and built a "natural landscape garden" around Longevity Hill (Wanshou Shan) and Kunming Lake on the western outskirts of Beijing. From Qianlong's "Garden of Clear Ripples" (Qingyi Yuan) to Cixi's "Summer Palace" (Yihe Yuan), this garden witnessed the burning and destruction of the Old Summer Palace in 1860 and was rebuilt in 1886 using funds diverted from the navy - the fate of a garden thus tightly bound to the rise and fall of a dynasty. The Summer Palace represents the pinnacle of Chinese garden art and is often called the "Museum of Royal Gardens." It was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1998.

· Location: No. 19 Xinjian Gongmen Road, Haidian District

· Opening Hours: Peak season 06:00-0:00 (site closes at 21:00); off-season 06:30-9:00 (site closes at 20:00). Opening hours for smaller sites inside the garden vary. Please check before your visit.

· Tickets: Peak season 30 RMB, off-season 20 RMB (entrance only); combined ticket peak season 60 RMB, off-season 50 RMB.

Beijing Central Axis

Seven point eight kilometers, running north to south through the old city - that is the length of the Beijing Central Axis, and also the ultimate scale of ancient Chinese capital planning. It began taking shape in the 13th century and matured in the 16th century. With the Bell and Drum Towers at its northern end, it runs south past Wanning Bridge, Jingshan (Prospect Hill), the Forbidden City, Duanmen (the End Gate), Tian'anmen, Tian'anmen Square and its surrounding buildings, Zhengyang Gate, the southern section road remains, and finally reaches Yongdingmen Gate. On July 27, 2024, UNESCO inscribed "Beijing Central Axis: A Masterpiece of the Ideal Capital Order in China" on the World Heritage List. Walking this line - from the morning bell and evening drum of the Bell and Drum Towers, to the echoes on the Circular Mound Altar, from the solemn majesty of the Forbidden City's three great halls to the towering Yongdingmen Gatehouse - the very spirit of Beijing lies around every corner along this axis.

Note: After the Central Axis was inscribed on the World Heritage List, several ancient buildings opened to the public for the first time. In 2024, the Shencang courtyard and Qingcheng Palace at the Xiannong Altar (Altar of Agriculture) both opened their gates - before that, Qingcheng Palace had not been open to ordinary people for 566 years.

· Location: The axis runs through Dongcheng and Xicheng districts, connecting the Bell and Drum Towers, Jingshan, the Forbidden City, Tian'anmen Square, Yongdingmen, and others.

· Opening Hours: Opening hours vary by individual heritage site. Please check each site's official announcement.

· Tickets: The Central Axis itself does not charge admission; each heritage site along the axis sells its own tickets.
