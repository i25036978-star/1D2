Beijing Cuisine

Beijing’s food culture began in the Liao and Jin dynasties and reached its peak during the six hundred years when the city served as the capital of the Yuan, Ming, and Qing empires. It blends the refinement of imperial cuisine, the wisdom of street food, and the bold flavors of multiple ethnic groups. With classics like Peking duck, instant-boiled mutton, douzhir (fermented mung bean drink), and lüzhua huoshao (braised offal stew), Beijing writes a living legend of “high elegance and down-to-earth taste.”This unique “flavor of old Beijing”makes the city a living museum of taste in the world’s culinary landscape.

Eight Classic Old Beijing

Peking duck is the culinary icon of Beijing, known for its glossy red color, crispy skin, and tender meat. Quanjude’s hanging oven roast duck and Bianyifang’s closed oven roast duck represent two major schools, each with its own merits. The duck is typically served with thin pancakes, sweet bean sauce, sliced scallions, and cucumber strips, rolled together by diners. The leftover duck bones can be made into soup or seasoned with salt and pepper - a true “one duck, three ways”experience.

· Recommended locations: Quanjude (Hepingmen) - No. 217, Qianmen Xiheyan, Xicheng District; Bianyifang (Xianyukou) - No. 65-77, Xianyukou Street, Dongcheng District

Douzhir is a fermented mung bean drink with a sour, slightly astringent taste - a unique acquired taste that true Beijingers love. It is typically served with crispy fried dough rings (jiaochang) and spicy pickled turnip strips. The combination of sour, salty, and crunchy flavors is a quintessential old Beijing breakfast experience.

· Recommended location: Huguosi Snacks (Huguosi main store) - No. 93 Huguosi Street, Xicheng District

Instant-Boiled Mutton (Lamb Hot Pot)

Old Beijing instant-boiled mutton is known for its simplicity: a pot of clear water with just a few slices of ginger and scallions. This lets the natural flavor of the lamb shine. Donglaishun’s hand-sliced mutton is as thin as paper and melts in the mouth. Dip it in a sauce made of sesame paste, fermented bean curd, and chives - it’s rich, savory, and perfect for cold winter days.

· Recommended location: Donglaishun (apm flagship store) - 5th floor, No. 138 Wangfujing Street, Dongcheng District

Lüzhua Huoshao

Lüzhua huoshao, often simply called “luzhu”，originated from the imperial “Suzhou-style braised pork”of the Qing court. The street version uses cheaper pork intestines and lungs, stewed with wheat cakes (huoshao) until the cakes soak up the rich, savory broth. Xiaochang Chen’s recipe has been passed down for a century and remains a beloved late-night comfort food.

· Recommended location: Xiaochang Chen Restaurant (Nanhengjie) - No. 194 Nanheng East Street, Xicheng District

Baodu is a classic Halal Beijing snack. Fresh lamb or beef tripe is blanched in boiling water for just a few seconds to preserve a crisp-tender texture. Baodu Feng has been serving this dish since the Guangxu era. The tripe is dipped in a mixture of sesame paste and chili oil - a perfect accompaniment to baijiu (Chinese liquor).

· Recommended location: Baodu Feng (Qianmen) - No. 56, Langfang Er Tiao, Menkuang Hutong, Qianmen Street, Xicheng District

Shaomai (Steamed Pork Dumplings)

Duyichu’s shaomai are known for their pomegranate-like shape and thin, translucent skins that don’t break. The filling is juicy and flavorful. The restaurant received its name from the Qianlong Emperor, who visited incognito one night and bestowed a plaque. Today, Duyichu’s shaomai-making technique is listed as a national intangible cultural heritage.

· Recommended location: Duyichu Shaomai (Qianmen) - No. 38 Qianmen Street, Dongcheng District

Zha jiang mian is the ultimate “everyday staple”of old Beijing - a bowl of simple pleasure that warms both the body and soul. Its history can be traced to the Ming Dynasty, when the famed Liubiju sauce shop was already perfecting the yellow soybean paste. By the Qing Dynasty, zha jiang mian had become known as the “noodle champion”of Beijing. The heart of the dish lies in the fried sauce: small diced pork belly is slowly simmered with fermented soybean paste over low heat until the oil and sauce separate, a traditional technique called “small bowl dry-frying.”The noodles are served with a generous platter of seasonal vegetable toppings - shredded cucumber, bean sprouts, radish strips - and a bowl of house-made pickled garlic on the side. Locals say: “No garlic with noodles? Might as well eat plain rice.”In winter, the noodles are served fresh from the pot - known as “pot-picked”- while in summer, they’re chilled before eating.

· Recommended locations: No. 69 Fangzhuanchang Zha Jiang Mian (Fangzhuanchang Hutong) - No. 1 Fangzhuanchang Hutong, Dongcheng District (Michelin Bib Gourmand recipient since 2020); Haiwanju (Zengguang Road) - No. 11 Zengguang Road, Haidian District

Wandouhuang and Aiwowo

Wandouhuang is a smooth, melt-in-your-mouth sweet pea cake that was once an imperial palace dessert. Aiwowo is made of glutinous rice wrapped around a sweet filling of red bean or sesame seeds, then rolled in shredded coconut or rice flour. Both are best enjoyed with a cup of tea for an authentic Beijing-style afternoon treat.

· Recommended location: Huguosi Snacks (Huguosi main store) - No. 93 Huguosi Street, Xicheng District; Fangshan Restaurant (Beihai Park) - Inside Beihai Park, Xicheng District

Two Modern Beijing Dishes

Naipi suan nai is a new sensation from the time-honored Beijing chain Ziguangyuan. Fresh milk is slowly fermented into a thick, creamy yogurt, and a delicate pale yellow milk skin forms naturally on top. In 2024, Ziguangyuan collaborated with the Forbidden City IP - the cup lid was printed with the Hall of Supreme Harmony and the playful slogan “Eat yogurt, then visit the palace”- turning it into an instant social media hit. Today, many other Beijing-style restaurants have launched their own versions, with daily sales reaching over 300,000 cups. It has been called “the new Beijing specialty.”

Shanzhan egan is one of the most iconic dishes of modern Beijing cuisine, first created by the Michelin-starred restaurant Shijiu. Inspired by traditional Chinese snacks, it uses thin hawthorn jelly sheets to wrap French foie gras, shaped like flower petals with an elegant presentation. The sweet-and-sour hawthorn perfectly balances the richness of the foie gras. On the palate, the fruity note arrives first, followed by the creamy, buttery texture of the foie gras. Today, this dish can be found in many new-style Beijing restaurants and is a must-order item.
