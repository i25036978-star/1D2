Beijing’s Cultural Vein

Peking Opera

Peking opera is the “national essence”of Beijing and the most significant form of traditional Chinese theatre. Its roots go back to 1790, the 55th year of the Qianlong reign of the Qing dynasty, when the Anhui opera troupe “Sanqing”arrived in Beijing to perform for Emperor Qianlong’s birthday. It was soon followed by three other Anhui troupes - “Sixi” “Hechun” and “Chuntai”- an event known as the “Four Great Anhui Troupes Entering Beijing.”After settling in the capital, these troupes absorbed elements from Han opera, Kunqu, Qin opera, and other regional styles. After decades of fusion and refinement, Peking opera as we know it today took shape by the mid-19th century. During the Tongzhi and Guangxu reigns, the painter Shen Rongpu created a portrait scroll titled Thirteen Absolute Masters of the Tongzhi and Guangxu Eras, capturing thirteen famous Peking opera and Kunqu performers, including Cheng Changgeng and Lu Shengkui. This scroll marked the coming of age of Peking opera. In the Republican era, the “Four Great Dan”- Mei Lanfang, Cheng Yanqiu, Xun Huisheng, and Shang Xiaoyun - brought female-role acting to its peak, and Peking opera spread across China and the world.

The art of Peking opera is built on strict conventions. The first is role categories - sheng (male), dan (female), jing (painted-face male), and chou (clown). Each category has its own techniques: the fan work of the xiaosheng (young male), the water sleeves of the dan, the acrobatics of the wusheng (military male), and the hair-swinging of the laosheng (old male). Then there are the “four skills and five methods”- singing, speaking, acting, and combat (the four skills); and hand, eye, body, step, and method (the five methods) - required training for every performer. Facial makeup (lianpu) is another signature feature: red for loyalty and courage, black for uprightness, white for treachery - a face is a character’s psychological profile. In performance, a whip stands for a galloping horse, four soldiers stand for an army of thousands, and a table and two chairs can be a throne hall or a barren mountain. This is the principle of “suggestiveness through emptiness”- using the fewest props to tell the richest stories.

In the Qing court, Empress Dowager Cixi was a devoted fan. She watched operas in the Yile Theatre of the Summer Palace for thirteen years, from 1895 to 1908, attending no fewer than two hundred performances. The imperial palace had three large opera stages; the largest was Qingyin Pavilion in the Chengde Mountain Resort, followed by Changyin Pavilion in the Forbidden City’s Palace of Tranquil Longevity, and then Yile Theatre in the Summer Palace. Outside the palace, theatres in the Dashilan area - Guanghelou, Sanqingyuan, Guangdelou - were packed with tea-drinking, melon-seed-cracking audiences. The spirit of old Beijing still lingers in the sounds of pihuang (the two main musical modes of Peking opera).

Beijing is a living museum. What you can see are the Forbidden City and the Great Wall. What you cannot see, but can hear and feel, are the arias in Peking opera, the punchlines in crosstalk, the spirit hidden behind a weathered red-lacquered door in a hutong, the dancing shadow figures between an artist’s fingers, and the enamel colours of cloisonné. These intangible things are the true foundation of this city.

· Recommendations: Dashilan area, Qianmen - the birthplace of Peking opera; Sanqingyuan and Guangdelou, historic theatres built in the Qing dynasty, still host performances today. Liyuan Theatre (Qianmen Jianguo Hotel) - performances year-round, suitable for first-time visitors. Mei Lanfang Memorial Museum (No. 9 Huguosi Street) and the former residence of Ma Lianliang (inside Chongwenmen) - former homes of Peking opera masters.

Crosstalk (Xiangsheng)

If Peking opera is Beijing’s “elegant music” crosstalk (xiangsheng) is its “earthy humour” No singing, no drums - just a mouth. Crosstalk is generally believed to have evolved from folk jokes during the Xianfeng reign of the Qing dynasty, about 150 years ago. It took root in Beijing because it matched the local character: fond of talking, fond of joking. Performance forms include single (one performer), duo (one lead, one support), and group (three or more). The classic duo has the “funny man”(dougen) telling jokes and the “straight man”(penggen) feeding lines - back and forth until the punchline lands.

The four basic skills of crosstalk are shuo (speaking), xue (imitating), dou (teasing), and chang (singing). Shuo is narration, setting up the story. Xue is mimicry - dialects, opera, bird calls. Dou is comic delivery - the punchline. Chang is not singing proper, but performing Taiping lyrics (a kind of folk shanty). A typical routine has four parts: dianhua (opening warm-up), piaoba (transition), huo (main story), and di (closing punchline). In the early days, performers wore long gowns and carried a wooden clapper and a folding fan as props. After the mid-20th century, the attire simplified, but the spirit of “Qiong Bu Pa”(Fear No Poverty) - the stage name of the art’s founder, Zhu Shaowen - has never been lost.

Famous crosstalk artists include Zhang Sanlu and Zhu Shaowen from the early days, then Li Dexi (stage name “Wanrenmi” meaning “Ten Thousand People’s Favourite” Jiao Dehai, Zhang Shouchen, Ma Sanli, then Hou Baolin, Liu Baorui, Ma Ji, and more recently Guo Degang and Yu Qian. Hou Baolin was a key figure. Born into the Manchu Bordered Blue Banner, he studied Peking opera as a child before switching to crosstalk. He elevated the art from street-corner ribaldry to true stage performance, greatly raising its status. Beijing crosstalk has always excelled at satire - wit and philosophy wrapped in laughter. Old Beijingers smile knowingly; outsiders laugh out loud.

· Recommendations: Tianqiao Theatre - one of the birthplaces of Beijing crosstalk; the original “street corner”tradition began here. Today, Deyunshe (Tianqiao) is located inside the Tianqiao Performing Arts Centre (No. 9 Tianqiao Nan Dajie, Xicheng District). Deyunshe (Tianqiao, Sanlitun) - the most popular crosstalk venues; book tickets in advance. Guangdelou Theatre (No. 39 Dashilan Street, Qianmen) - one of the oldest surviving theatres in Beijing, dating to the first year of the Jiaqing reign of the Qing dynasty (1796); Guo Degang performed here in his early years.

Hutongs and Siheyuan (Courtyard Houses)

Hutongs and siheyuan are the “cells”of Beijing. Old Beijingers say: “Hutongs are Beijing’s roots; siheyuan are Beijing’s soul.”As early as the Yuan dynasty, when Kublai Khan built Dadu (Great Capital), a regular grid of neighbourhoods was planned. The alleys from Xisi North First to Eighth Streets date from that time - their layout unchanged for over 700 years. The word “hutong”comes from Mongolian, meaning “small lane.”Beijing once had more than 7,000 hutongs, each with its own story.

The oldest surviving hutong is Brick Pagoda Hutong (Zhuan Ta Hutong), built in the Yuan dynasty. It is the only hutong in Beijing that has been continuously documented from the Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties through the Republican era, and is called “the root of Beijing’s hutongs.”In the Yuan period, it was a lively entertainment district with theatres and teahouses where Yuan opera was performed. The writer Lu Xun also lived here and wrote about its street life. Today, the “Root of Hutongs”cultural experience centre has opened, with a tea-coffee space, a themed post office, and souvenirs like a 3D pop-up book of Beijing courtyard houses.

If hutongs are the skeleton, siheyuan are the flesh and blood. A standard siheyuan faces south, with main rooms (zhengfang), side rooms (xiangfang), and opposite rooms (daozuofang) enclosing a square courtyard. The hierarchy is strict: “north room is the seat of honour, side rooms are secondary, opposite room is guest.”Gate towers, stone door blocks, couplets on the gate, spirit screens, brick carvings - every part has its own rules. The number of door pins (menzan) on the gate tells the owner’s official rank. Living in a siheyuan, summers are spent in the shade of a jujube tree in the courtyard; winters are spent inside listening to the north wind howl. That is how Beijingers lived, generation after generation.

· Recommendations: Xisi North First to Eighth Streets - among the first batch of historical and cultural preservation zones in Beijing. They retain the Yuan-dynasty lane layout and Ming-Qing style courtyard clusters, with little over-commercialisation and few tourists - ideal for a quiet experience of old Beijing’s unhurried rhythm. Brick Pagoda Hutong - “Root of Beijing’s Hutongs” the “Root of Hutongs”cultural experience centre is now open, including a tea space and a themed post office. Shijia Hutong (Dongcheng District) - about 700 metres long, home to dozens of famous figures throughout history. It has well-preserved courtyard houses and an authentic small-lane atmosphere; walking here you can feel the warmth and hospitality of Beijingers.

Shadow Puppetry (Piyingxi)

Shadow puppetry is a “beam of light”in the memory of old Beijing - an oil lamp, a white cloth, a few cow-hide puppets, and the dancing fingers of an artist, telling the joys and sorrows of human existence. The history of Beijing shadow puppetry can be traced to the Zhengde reign of the Ming dynasty. There were once eastern and western schools, but the eastern school has long been lost. The surviving western school is the only remaining lineage of Beijing shadow puppetry.

The real continuity of the western school begins in 1842, when a Manchu named Lu Decheng founded the “Xiangshun Shadow Play Troupe”in Beijing. This marked the beginning of the Lu family’s shadow puppetry legacy, which has lasted for more than 380 years. The Lu family troupe originated in the Shunzhi reign of the Qing dynasty (1644). They moved to Beijing after the Manchu entry through Shanhaiguan and settled in Changping. The art has been passed down through six generations. Their style incorporates elements of Peking opera and Kunqu, forming a unique “Lu-style”performance system. The fourth-generation heir, Lu Zongyou, reformed the vocal style and made the first shadow-puppetry gramophone recording. The fifth-generation heir, Lu Jingda, innovated the carving technique; his works have been included in professional anthologies. The Lu family troupe collaborated with the Peking opera master Qiu Shengrong to create new performance forms, undertook many state-level foreign cultural exchange missions, and even performed for soldiers on the front lines in the Korean War.

The greatest characteristic of Beijing shadow puppetry is that it absorbs the vocal styles of Peking opera, Kunqu, and narrative singing, forming its own unique school. The puppetry is delicate and expressive - puppets can look into a mirror, shed tears, and apply makeup with convincing realism. In 1957, the Lu family troupe was reorganised as the Beijing Xuanwu Shadow Puppet Troupe (later renamed the Beijing Shadow Puppet Troupe), the only professional shadow puppet company in Beijing. The sixth-generation heirs, Lu Hai and Lu Lianda, still perform today. The seventh-generation heir, Li Bin, has formed a troupe of little people (dwarf actors), continuing the tradition in a new way.

· Recommendations: Beijing Shadow Puppet Troupe (Xicheng District) - the only professional shadow puppet company in Beijing, direct heirs of the Lu family tradition. Regular performances and hands-on activities. Check the schedule in advance. Heping Guojing (Beijing Hutong Cultural Experience Base) (Basement level 2, Wangfujing Department Store, Dongcheng District) - step into recreated old Beijing scenes such as Meichang Hutong and Xiaojue Temple; experience traditional Beijing culture including shadow puppetry and dough figurines. Beijing Intangible Cultural Heritage Experience Centre (Xicheng District) - housed in a well-preserved late-Qing siheyuan (district-level protected heritage site); offers opportunities to learn about shadow puppetry and other traditional crafts.

Cloisonné (Jingtailan)

Cloisonné, whose Chinese name means “copper-body filigree enamel” is a decorative craft using copper as the base, fine copper wire to create patterns, and coloured enamel glaze to fill the spaces, followed by firing, polishing, and gold plating. What sets it apart from other traditional crafts is that from its very birth, it was almost exclusively a royal art - never leaving the imperial palace.

The history of cloisonné in China can be traced to the Yuan dynasty, when enamelware from Western Asia entered China through trade with the Arab world. During the Jingtai reign of the Ming dynasty (1450-1457), under imperial patronage, enamel techniques matured, especially the breakthrough in blue glazes. Later generations gave this craft a resounding name - “Jingtai blue”(Jingtailan). During the Kangxi and Qianlong reigns of the Qing dynasty, cloisonné reached its peak. At the New Year’s Eve banquet in the 44th year of the Qianlong reign (1779), all court officials used porcelain bowls, but only the Qianlong Emperor himself used a set of cloisonné tableware - a testament to its supreme status.

The production process is extremely complex. A finished piece requires seven major steps: design, base making, filigree (wire inlay), enamel filling, firing, polishing, and gilding - nearly a hundred sub-operations, taking three months and more than a dozen artisans to complete. “Without white bletilla the flower will not bloom; without the butterfly dance of the eight trigrams it is hard to emerge; without the hardship of water and stone grinding, how can spring be forever preserved?”This folk rhyme speaks to the difficulty of making cloisonné. No wonder there is an old saying: “One piece of cloisonné is worth ten pieces of official kiln porcelain”.

The craft went through difficult times in the late Qing and Republican periods, when social upheaval nearly extinguished it. After the founding of the People’s Republic of China, the architects Liang Sicheng and Lin Huiyin formed a craft group in the Architecture Department of Tsinghua University, advocating the rescue and revival of cloisonné and carrying out innovative designs. Today, the Beijing Enamel Factory in Dongcheng District brings together two-thirds of the national-level master craftsmen of cloisonné, giving this ancient art new life. In 2006, cloisonné making was inscribed on the first national list of intangible cultural heritage. At the 2014 APEC summit, a cloisonné vase titled Four Seas Rising was presented as a state gift to the leaders of each APEC economy - a court craft from the Yuan dynasty, once again standing before the world.

· Recommendations: Beijing Enamel Factory (No. 10 Anlelin Road, Yongwai, Dongcheng District) - the leading enterprise in China’s cloisonné industry. It houses the Cloisonné Art Museum, and visitors can watch the production workshops, see masters at work (wire inlay, enamel filling), and even try hands-on activities. Qianmen Dashilan (Dongcheng District) - a famous historic commercial street on the Central Axis, with many intangible cultural heritage shops. You can browse and purchase shadow puppetry, Beijing fans, clay rabbit gods, cloisonné, and other traditional Beijing crafts.
